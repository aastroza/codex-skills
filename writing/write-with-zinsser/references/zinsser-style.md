# Zinsser Style Reference

Use this reference when drafting, editing, or critiquing prose.

## Core rules

- Strip each sentence to its cleanest form. Every word must earn its place.
- Start with the point. Cut openings such as "in this post" and "it is worth noting".
- Use verbs instead of noun clusters. Change "made a decision" to "decided".
- Cut clutter: qualifiers, filler phrases, repeated ideas, and obvious setup.
- Prefer active voice.
- Use concrete detail only when the source gives it. Do not invent numbers or examples to make a sentence feel stronger.
- Keep sentences and paragraphs short. One sentence should do one job.
- Use first person, second person, or third person with intent. Do not drift.
- Avoid corrective constructions. Say the true thing directly.
- Avoid em dashes. Use periods, commas, colons, or parentheses.
- Replace dead openings such as "there are" and "it is important to" with the subject.
- Cut weasel words unless they carry a real limit.
- Use strong verbs. "The function validates" beats "the function does validation".
- Keep tense, voice, and mood stable.
- Keep lists parallel.
- Vary sentence length for rhythm, but do not let the rhythm become cute.

## Honest prose

- Skip hype, inflated claims, empty superlatives, and fake urgency.
- Stick to the source. If the source does not say it, omit it or mark it as a question.
- Say what you know. Say what you do not know when that matters.
- Prefer small words: use, buy, help, start, fix, build.
- Leave the writer in the piece. Keep surprise, curiosity, mild frustration, humor, and judgment when they are grounded in the work.

## AI-assisted writing workflow

1. Gather raw material first. Voice notes, messy bullets, screenshots, logs, and source documents give the draft texture.
2. Build context before structure. Find the claim, scene, problem, and useful tension.
3. Draft with the style guide in view.
4. Edit in three passes: structure, sentences, AI tells.
5. Polish by hand for voice, argument, and taste.
6. Critique the draft as a strict editor. Look for clutter, fake confidence, and missing story.

The useful split: AI can organize, draft, and find clutter. The human owns voice, judgment, story, and taste.

## Structural patterns

- Trust the story. If an example made the point, do not summarize it again.
- Weave definitions into prose. Definition boxes can stop the reader.
- Use categories only when each category earns its place with an example.
- Do not pre-argue the next section. Point forward only when it helps.
- Put rules before examples when they frame the example. Cut rules that repeat the example.
- Make transitions specific. Name what the next section solves.
- Pair failure stories with success stories so the reader sees what good looks like.
- Follow interest, not taxonomy. A tool's internal structure rarely matches the reader's curiosity.
- Make each example serve the current argument. Link elsewhere for a different story.

## Recurring critique patterns

- Double-stating: the draft says the idea, then says it again. Keep the stronger version.
- Trailing off: the draft reaches an interesting moment, then moves on. Stay there.
- Transcript residue: spoken loops and half-thoughts survived the edit.
- List architecture without narrative: sections can be reordered without changing the piece. Add a thread or cut.
- Repetitive journey: the same "tried, failed" beat repeats. Compress the pattern once the reader gets it.
- Forced connection: two unrelated ideas get tied together because the ending wanted symmetry. Let them stand apart.

## Technical details

Technical details must explain a choice, a trade-off, a constraint, or a surprise.

Keep:

- "I picked CodeMirror because it handles markdown well, it is extensible, and its styling model gave me the look I wanted."
- "The sidebar checks for one folder. No commercial product would ship that, but this is a personal tool."

Cut or rewrite:

- "It uses FSEvents, not polling, with a debounce threshold."
- "The bundle is 25KB across 8 files."
- "Available as a Swift package."

Lead with what the reader experiences or what the writer learned. Use mechanism only when the mechanism matters.

## Before and after

Throat-clearing:

- Before: "Learning to edit is perhaps the most important skill to develop when working with AI writing tools."
- After: "Editing is the most important skill for AI writing."

Nounism:

- Before: "The implementation of the feature required modification of the configuration."
- After: "Implementing the feature required modifying the config."

Clutter:

- Before: "The main dashboard is now smarter. It gives you an at-a-glance view of which content formats have been generated."
- After: "The dashboard shows which formats have been generated for each project."

Specificity:

- Before: "Performance improved significantly."
- After: "Response time dropped from 2 seconds to 200 ms." Use this only when the source gives the numbers.
